About C*Power v0.1

What is C*Power?

C*Power is a browser-based, mobile-friendly clone of G*Power, generated by a large language model (Claude by Anthropic). It requires no installation and runs entirely in your browser — including on smartphones and tablets.

⚠️ WORK IN PROGRESS: Calculations in this tool have NOT been independently validated against G*Power or other statistical software. Results may contain errors. Do not rely on this tool for published research without cross-checking against a validated tool.

References cited in the parameter tooltips

The effect size benchmarks and conventions shown when you hover the ⓘ icons next to input parameters are drawn from:

  • Cohen, J. (1988). Statistical Power Analysis for the Behavioral Sciences (2nd ed.). Hillsdale, NJ: Lawrence Erlbaum. — The classical reference for "small / medium / large" conventions for d, f, w, h, f², r.
  • Gignac, G. E., & Szodorai, E. T. (2016). Effect size guidelines for individual differences researchers. Personality and Individual Differences, 102, 74–78. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.paid.2016.06.069 — Empirical distribution of effect sizes (median r ≈ 0.19, median d ≈ 0.36).
  • Lakens, D. (2013). Calculating and reporting effect sizes to facilitate cumulative science: A practical primer for t-tests and ANOVAs. Frontiers in Psychology, 4, 863. https://doi.org/10.3389/fpsyg.2013.00863 — Practical guide to Cohen's d, dz, d_av and η²/ω².
  • Lakens, D., & Evers, E. R. K. (2014). Sailing from the seas of chaos into the corridor of stability: Practical recommendations to increase the informational value of studies. Perspectives on Psychological Science, 9(3), 278–292. — Recommends targeting power ≥ 0.90.
  • Hattie, J. (2009). Visible Learning: A synthesis of over 800 meta-analyses relating to achievement. London: Routledge. — Identifies d ≈ 0.40 as the educational "hinge point".
  • Ioannidis, J. P. A. (2005). Why most published research findings are false. PLoS Medicine, 2(8), e124. — On false discovery rates & the cost of low power.
  • Benjamin, D. J., et al. (2018). Redefine statistical significance. Nature Human Behaviour, 2, 6–10. — Proposed lowering α to 0.005 for new findings.
  • Lorah, J. (2018). Effect size measures for multilevel models: definition, interpretation, and TIMSS example. Large-scale Assessments in Education, 6, 8. — Domain-specific effect size benchmarks beyond Cohen.
  • Cumming, G. (2014). The new statistics: Why and how. Psychological Science, 25(1), 7–29. — On reporting effect sizes and confidence intervals.

Statistical methods used

  • Normal CDF: Abramowitz & Stegun (1964) rational approximation; inverse via Beasley-Springer-Moro algorithm.
  • t, F, χ² CDFs: regularised incomplete beta and gamma functions (Lentz continued fraction; series + continued fraction).
  • Noncentral F: Poisson-weighted mixture of central F distributions.
  • Noncentral t: shifted approximation (normal-equivalent for moderate-to-large df).
  • Noncentral χ²: Sankaran-style Wilson-Hilferty approximation.
  • Sample-size search: stepwise integer search starting from minimum valid N (df ≥ 1).
  • Sensitivity / criterion analyses: bisection on bracketed monotone power function.
